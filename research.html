Research

Our laboratory employs mouse models, cellular immunology, functional genomics, and single-cell profiling to understand how cancers evade the immune system and to identify new targets to enhance immune responses to tumors.

In Vivo CRISPR-Based Genetic Screens

We employ large-scale animal-based genetic screening to discover mechanisms controlling tumor responses to immunotherapy. Using pooled in vivo CRISPR screens in mouse tumor models, we can systematically identify genes that regulate sensitivity and resistance to immune checkpoint blockade.

A key finding from our work: deletion of PTPN2, a phosphatase that negatively regulates interferon gamma sensing, markedly enhances tumor sensitivity to PD-1 checkpoint blockade. This discovery opened a new avenue for combination immunotherapy strategies.

We continue to apply and refine this platform to map the landscape of immune evasion pathways and identify novel therapeutic targets across multiple cancer types.

Overcoming Acquired Resistance to Immunotherapy

Many patients who initially respond to immune checkpoint blockade eventually develop acquired resistance. Understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying this resistance is critical for developing strategies to restore immune sensitivity.

We discovered that loss of ADAR1, an adenosine deaminase acting on RNA, inflames the tumor microenvironment and can overcome resistance to immune checkpoint blockade. ADAR1 normally suppresses innate immune sensing of endogenous double-stranded RNA; its loss unleashes this sensing pathway, creating a more immunostimulatory tumor environment.

This work has broad implications for treating patients who fail to respond to or relapse on checkpoint immunotherapy.

Antigen Presentation Research

Antigen Presentation in Immunotherapy

Efficient presentation of tumor-derived peptides via major histocompatibility complexes (MHC) is essential for adaptive immune recognition of cancer cells. Tumors frequently downregulate antigen presentation machinery as a mechanism of immune evasion.

We investigate how cellular-derived peptide processing and MHC presentation shapes tumor–immune cell interactions. Using our in vivo CRISPR platform and biochemical approaches, we are uncovering novel regulators of this pathway and developing strategies to restore or enhance antigen presentation in tumors.

Recent work identified ERAP, an aminopeptidase involved in MHC-I peptide trimming, as a target whose inhibition enhances antitumor immunity by altering the repertoire of presented antigens.
